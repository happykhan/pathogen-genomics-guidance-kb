# Analysis, Reporting, and Decision Use

## Purpose

Describe how pathogen genomics analysis and reports connect to public-health action.

## Guidance Draft

Pathogen genomics analysis is only useful when it changes what public-health teams can see or do. Sequence data can identify organisms, detect clusters, refine outbreak case definitions, infer relatedness, support antimicrobial-resistance surveillance, monitor viral evolution, and help target interventions. The analysis pipeline is therefore part of a larger decision system, not an end in itself.

Armstrong et al. show the breadth of public-health use. They describe pathogen genomics contributing to foodborne outbreak investigation, tuberculosis control, influenza vaccine-strain selection, malaria drug-resistance surveillance, parasitic disease investigation, HIV molecular surveillance, and antimicrobial-resistance work. The paper also stresses that genomic data need integration with epidemiological data to realise their value. A tree, cluster, or resistance marker has more public-health meaning when linked to time, place, sample source, exposure, and case information.

The PHE case study gives concrete reporting and decision-use examples. The central service combined sequence-analysis data with LIMS information to produce final sample reports. WGS reduced the Salmonella workflow from conventional typing that could take around 20 days for serotyping plus additional molecular typing to a WGS service that generated reports within five working days. The case study links this reporting model to outbreak action: ruling cases in or out more accurately, linking geographically dispersed cases, identifying sources, and supporting control measures.

The same source shows why reporting is not just a file-delivery problem. PHE invested in user engagement, roadshows, and communication about how WGS information would be returned and understood. This matters for a guidance chapter. Reports should be designed for their users: reference laboratories, health protection teams, epidemiologists, food-safety agencies, hospitals, and international partners may need different levels of detail.

A useful pathogen genomics report should separate technical outputs from interpreted public-health conclusions. The current sources support the distinction but do not yet define a reporting standard. Technical outputs may include organism identity, sequence type, resistance markers, QC status, relatedness metrics, cluster assignment, or phylogenetic placement. Public-health conclusions may include whether cases are plausibly linked, whether further investigation is needed, whether a source is supported, or whether a control measure should be considered. Those conclusions require epidemiological context and domain review.

This chapter needs more disease-programme sources before it can give detailed interpretation rules. The current evidence can support a general principle: analysis and reporting should be designed backwards from the public-health decision, with enough provenance and context for users to understand what the result can and cannot support.

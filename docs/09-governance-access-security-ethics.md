# Governance, Access, Security, and Ethics

## Purpose

Describe the current source-backed material on identity, access, data sharing risk, and governance questions for pathogen genomics systems.

## Guidance Draft

Pathogen genomics systems need clear rules about who can see, upload, analyse, interpret, export, share, and delete data. These rules are not only technical access settings. They connect laboratory operations, central IT, cybersecurity, information governance, public-health law, research collaboration, and international data sharing.

The PHA4GE source gives the strongest current material on identity and access management. It frames IAM as a continuous responsibility that includes provisioning, authentication, authorisation, role assignment, access review, offboarding, monitoring, and auditability. It also notes that genomic compute infrastructure often requires coordination between the laboratory, institutional IT, external providers, cybersecurity, and information governance.

That is a useful starting point because access changes over time. New users join, staff leave, outbreak teams form, collaborators need temporary access, external platforms are used, and data move from local systems into shared repositories. A genomics programme needs a process for those changes, not only a list of usernames.

The source material also supports a risk-based view of data sharing. Armstrong et al. describe public-health value from near real-time sharing through NCBI-hosted pathogen databases and global platforms, but they also state that openness cannot be complete and unconditional because agencies must consider confidentiality and risk. The PHE case study raises similar future-service questions: how data are collected and shared, who has oversight, who has access, and for what purpose.

The current KB can therefore support three basic governance messages. First, IAM must be planned as part of infrastructure. Second, data sharing needs explicit decisions about access, purpose, timing, and risk. Third, central IT and information-governance teams need to understand genomic data flows before incidents or disputes arise.

The chapter is not yet complete. It does not yet have enough source material on privacy law, ethical review, benefit sharing, cross-border sharing, trusted research environments, access agreements, or security baselines. WHO data-sharing principles, WHO Pandemic Hub platform principles, UKHSA data-sharing policy, ECDC cross-border surveillance guidance, and national policy sources should be extracted before this becomes formal guidance.

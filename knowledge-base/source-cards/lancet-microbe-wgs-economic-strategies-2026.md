## Relevance

This Personal View directly addresses the economic strategy question for pathogen WGS: whether programmes should invest in continuous genomic surveillance or rely on outbreak-triggered sequencing. It is especially relevant to the guide's leadership-facing value argument because it frames WGS as an investment decision with trade-offs between steady surveillance costs and the risk of delayed outbreak detection.

## Extracted Notes

- The paper contrasts comprehensive or selective routine WGS surveillance with sequencing initiated only after an outbreak is suspected.
- Continuous surveillance has higher upfront and recurring costs, but can detect clusters and transmission events earlier and may avert large outbreak costs.
- Outbreak-triggered sequencing lowers routine expenditure, but can miss cryptic transmission and can allow outbreaks to grow before control measures are triggered.
- The paper cautions that returns depend on setting, pathogen, health-care system, sequencing approach, sampling strategy, and economic resources.
- The paper treats selective surveillance as an important middle ground, but emphasises the need for explicit criteria for which isolates or infections are sequenced.
- It reinforces that sequencing data must be linked to epidemiological investigation, source identification, control action, data sharing, and sustained funding.

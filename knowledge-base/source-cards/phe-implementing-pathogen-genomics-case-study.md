## Extracted/Adapted Text

This case study is highly relevant for implementation detail. It describes Public Health England's establishment of a centralised WGS service at Colindale and the transition of the Gastrointestinal Bacteria Reference Unit to WGS-led reference microbiology for major gastrointestinal pathogens.

The source supports lifecycle and workflow content. It describes receipt of extracted DNA, sequencing, robotic library preparation and quality checks, LIMS-based sample information, storage and analysis on IT/bioinformatics infrastructure, integration with LIMS data, and production of final reports for service users.

The source supports implementation planning. It describes business-case development, capital planning, laboratory-space remodelling, health-and-safety review, equipment and IT procurement, workforce/training review, user requirements, sample quality/quantity expectations, turnaround-time requirements, and decisions about whether users receive raw sequence data or analysed pipeline outputs.

The source supports infrastructure detail. It identifies the need for sequencing equipment, robotics, IT infrastructure, compute clusters, high-performance storage, archive storage, data-lifecycle management software, LIMS, and enough processing speed and storage capacity to avoid service impact while remaining scalable.

The source strongly supports quality-management and validation content. Before traditional testing was switched off, PHE ran conventional methods and WGS workflows in parallel, compared datasets, performed gap analysis, adjusted protocols, established quality parameters across WGS typing steps, and assessed input and output material. For Salmonella, validation compared 1,538 representative samples in phase I and 6,887 isolates in phase II, with 97% concordance between WGS and conventional methods.

The source supports workforce content. It describes training needs for laboratory staff, specialist bioinformatics staff, computing/software development expertise, cross-training for epidemiologists and clinical scientists, user engagement, roadshows, and multidisciplinary collaboration across laboratory, epidemiology, bioinformatics, food safety, hospitals, and external agencies.

The source supports accreditation content. It reports ISO 15189 accreditation for the central sequencing service and reference laboratory WGS services, while noting that applying accreditation standards to bioinformatic pipelines was a new challenge.

The source supports public-health impact and reporting content. It reports a five-day sample-submission-to-final-report service, replacement of at least 10 validated conventional processes, reduced sample handling, reduced staff costs, reallocation of staff toward data analysis and interpretation, and outbreak examples where WGS improved case definitions, source attribution, and interventions.

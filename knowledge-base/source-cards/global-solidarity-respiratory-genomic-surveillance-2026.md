# Source Card: Global Solidarity in Respiratory Virus Genomic Surveillance

## Source Basis

- de Jong SPJ, Nichols BE, de Ruijter A, Parker E, Mitesser V, Happi C, et al. Global solidarity in genomic surveillance improves early detection of acute respiratory virus threats. *Nature Communications*. 2026;17:765. doi:10.1038/s41467-025-67442-9.

## Relevance

This modelling study supports the equity and distributed-capability argument for genomic surveillance: expanding capacity only in already high-capacity countries does not deliver the same global early-detection benefit as minimum capacity across countries.

## Useful For

- Why genomics and why distributed capability.
- Respiratory-virus surveillance.
- Global health security and equity.
- Investment case for minimum national sequencing capacity.
- Surveillance regularity and reporting timeliness.

## Extracted Notes

- The abstract states that a minimum sequencing capacity of two sequences per million people per week, with fortnightly regularity, could reduce time to first detection of novel respiratory virus threats by weeks to months compared with COVID-19-era global sequencing patterns.
- The study argues that benefits cannot be achieved by siloed expansion in countries already possessing strong capacity.

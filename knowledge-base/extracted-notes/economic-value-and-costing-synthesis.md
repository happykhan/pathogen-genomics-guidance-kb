## Relevant Extracted Points

### What The Economic Case Can Say

- Economic value should be tied to a defined service model and public-health use case, not to genomics as a generic technology.
- PHE provides a service-transition case: WGS replaced or consolidated older reference-laboratory processes, changed staff effort, required validation/accreditation and user engagement, and created new analysis and interpretation work.
- GenomeTrakr provides a food-safety-specific economic evaluation linking WGS source tracking and public database growth to estimated reductions in outbreak illnesses and large annual health benefits relative to public-health-agency investment.
- Egli and Howden frame the central strategic trade-off: continuous WGS surveillance has higher routine costs but can detect clusters earlier; outbreak-triggered sequencing lowers routine expenditure but can miss cryptic transmission and delay control.
- The national investment case source supports the need to make an explicit investment case that connects public-health benefits, system requirements, and financing.

### What The Economic Case Should Not Overclaim

- Economic findings from one pathogen, country, surveillance design, or response system should not be presented as universally generalisable.
- Return on investment depends on sampling strategy, pathogen biology, disease burden, outbreak cost, response capacity, reporting timeliness, staff capacity, and whether sequence results change action.
- Genomics can consolidate some processes, but it also creates recurring costs for storage, compute, workflow maintenance, validation, quality systems, specialist staff, training, data sharing, and user support.

### Cost Categories For Planning

- WHO's costing tool provides the strongest current category model for practical planning: throughput, platform, reagents and consumables, equipment, personnel and training, facilities and transport, bioinformatics, and quality management.
- Bioinformatics costs should include cloud, in-house server, or hybrid arrangements; software licensing; storage; hardware; and maintenance fees.
- Costing should be a scenario exercise. A programme should compare throughput assumptions, platform choices, staffing models, quality-management scope, data storage, compute model, transport, procurement, and whether costs can be shared across pathogen programmes.

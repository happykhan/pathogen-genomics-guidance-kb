## Relevant Extracted Points

### Clinical Microbiology Implementation

- The clinical implementation review distinguishes clinical microbiology laboratories from public-health laboratories, noting direct patient-care responsibilities, diagnostic reporting expectations, and regulatory obligations.
- It lists key implementation considerations including use cases, staffing, competencies, sequencing platform selection, laboratory setup, SOPs, bioinformatics analysis, storage, transfer, sharing, reporting, interpretation, quality control, validation, verification, external quality assurance, and implementation model.
- It describes centralised reference laboratory, decentralised in-house, hybrid, and collaborative models.
- It emphasises reporting as a specialised skill involving written reports, interpretive commentary, and verbal discussions with clinicians or infection-control teams.
- It identifies barriers including cost, infrastructure, workflow requirements, staffing and training challenges, bioinformatics implementation, regulatory requirements, lack of standardised quality systems, and interpretive uncertainty.

### East African Community Landscape

- The East African Community assessment documents major disparities in bacterial pathogen sequencing and analysis capacity for AMR surveillance and outbreak response.
- It reports heavy reliance on third-party organisations for bacterial NGS and identifies insufficient local sequencing facilities, limited bioinformatics expertise, inadequate compute resources, and inadequate data-sharing mechanisms as major barriers.
- It argues for equipment, technology, capacity building, regional collaboration, harmonised standards, long-term training, and stronger in-country genomic and bioinformatics capacity.
- The paper highlights that reliance on external sequencing and analysis can delay real-time surveillance decisions and limit direct local interpretation.
